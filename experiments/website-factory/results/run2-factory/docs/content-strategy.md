## 1. Messaging Hierarchy

### Primary Message

> **Sleep smarter, think sharper.**

This is the brand tagline and the single idea the entire site must reinforce. It is a cause-and-effect promise: better sleep is not a wellness goal in isolation — it is a cognitive performance strategy. Every headline, body paragraph, and CTA should trace back to this claim.

### Secondary Messages

These three supporting ideas operationalize the primary message and map directly to the features users care about most.

**1. Your sleep data, connected to your work.**
NightOwl is not another sleep score. It speaks the language of knowledge workers: commits, deadlines, focus blocks, cognitive load. It connects what happens at night to what happens at your desk.

**2. Passive tracking, active insights.**
You do not change your routine to use NightOwl. It runs in the background, learns your patterns, and surfaces insights when they are useful — not as raw data dumps, but as specific, actionable guidance.

**3. Private by design. Powerful by default.**
For individuals, your data stays yours. For teams, aggregate-only insights respect each member's privacy while giving managers the signal they need to prevent burnout.

### Supporting Messages (Proof Points)

These are the third-tier claims used in feature descriptions, FAQs, and microcopy. They substantiate the secondary messages with specificity.

- "Correlates sleep quality with coding output, writing volume, and focus session length."
- "No manual logging. Works with the wearable you already own."
- "Integrates with GitHub, Linear, Notion, Toggl, and 20+ tools — so insights live where your work happens."
- "Individual data is never shared with your team, manager, or employer."
- "Export raw data as CSV or JSON for your own analysis."
- "Chronotype-aware scheduling recommendations that match your actual rhythm, not a generic 8-hour prescription."
- "Team Insights shows aggregate patterns — never individual sleep data."

## 2. Tone of Voice Guidelines

### The Governing Principle

NightOwl speaks like a brilliant colleague who happens to have a PhD in sleep science: intellectually rigorous, but conversational. It does not lecture. It does not use wellness-industry softness ("nourish your rest!") or clinical detachment ("REM latency was reduced by 14.3 minutes"). It says something true, specific, and useful — and trusts the reader to be smart enough to care about it.

### Four Tone Dimensions

**Intelligent, not jargon-heavy**
Use precise language, but define or illustrate it immediately. Trust the reader's intelligence without abandoning them to acronyms or clinical terminology.

- Do: "Deep sleep consolidates what you learned today — and NightOwl tracks how much you're actually getting."
- Avoid: "NightOwl monitors NREM Stage 3 slow-wave sleep polysomnographic indices."

**Data-driven, not clinical**
Numbers and specificity build trust. But they should feel like evidence for a point, not a data dump.

- Do: "Engineers who average under 6 hours of sleep show 40% more bug introductions in code review the following day."
- Avoid: "Studies indicate a statistically significant inverse correlation (p < 0.05) between sleep duration and error frequency."

**Direct, not pushy**
NightOwl earns attention by being useful. CTAs invite, not demand. There is no artificial urgency or manipulative framing.

- Do: "See what your data reveals." / "Try it free for 14 days."
- Avoid: "Don't miss out — limited spots available!" / "You need this."

**Warm, not casual**
The voice has humanity and a quiet confidence. It is not a startup trying to sound relatable by being glib. It is not a corporation being stiff.

- Do: "Most people know they should sleep more. NightOwl shows you why it actually matters for the work you care about."
- Avoid: "Sleep is SO important (we know, we know)." / "Pursuant to optimal cognitive functioning strategies..."

## 3. Section-by-Section Content Outline

**Goal:** Establish the core promise in under five seconds. Qualify the audience immediately. Drive to the primary CTA.

**Headline:**

> Sleep smarter, think sharper.

- "NightOwl connects your sleep quality to your cognitive performance — so you can make decisions backed by your own data, not generic advice."

- "You track everything else. Finally, a sleep platform built for the way you work."

**Body copy (20-30 words max):**

> Passive sleep tracking that integrates with GitHub, Linear, and Notion. Personalized insights for engineers, data scientists, researchers, and writers who need their brain working at its best.

**Primary CTA:** "Start tracking free — 14 days, no card required"
**Secondary CTA:** "See how it works" (scrolls to How It Works)

**Tone notes:** Confident, specific. Name-drop the job titles and tools in the first paragraph — this qualifies visitors immediately and signals "this is for you" to the target audience.

### Section 7: FAQ

**Goal:** Eliminate the friction that prevents sign-up. Address the actual objections from knowledge workers in this target audience, not generic SaaS FAQ content.

**Section headline:**

> Questions from people who ask good questions.

**FAQ items:**

**Q: Do I need a specific wearable device?**
A: NightOwl works with Apple Watch, Oura Ring, Fitbit, Garmin, and Whoop. If you already own one of these, you're ready. We're adding support for additional devices — check our integration page for the current list.

**Q: What work data does NightOwl actually read?**
A: Only what you explicitly connect and authorize. For GitHub, that means commit timestamps and PR activity — not code content. For Linear or Jira, it reads task completion timestamps. NightOwl never reads the content of your work, only the timing and volume signals you choose to share.

**Q: I'm an engineering manager. Can I see my team members' individual sleep data?**
A: No. And this is intentional, not an oversight. Team Insights shows aggregated, anonymized patterns across the team. Individual data is anonymized at the source and is never surfaced to any admin, manager, or employer. Ever.

**Q: Can I export my raw data?**
A: Yes. Pro and Team plan users can export full sleep datasets in CSV or JSON format at any time. You own your data. We provide an API for direct programmatic access on the Pro plan.

**Q: How is this different from the sleep app that came with my wearable?**
A: Wearable sleep apps show you sleep data in isolation — a score, a graph, a generic insight. NightOwl correlates that data with your actual work output to answer a different question: not "how did you sleep?" but "what does that sleep mean for your performance today and your patterns over time?" It also aggregates across multiple wearables if you switch devices.

**Q: What if I don't change anything after seeing the data?**
A: That's fine. Understanding your patterns is valuable even if you don't immediately change your habits. Many users report that just having the data changes their relationship to late-night work sessions — seeing the correlation makes the tradeoff feel real in a way that advice never did.

**Q: Is my health data secure?**
A: Sleep data is encrypted at rest and in transit. We do not sell data to advertisers, insurers, or third parties. We do not share individual data with employers. Our privacy policy is written in plain English — not because we're legally required to, but because we work with health data and believe transparency is non-negotiable. You can delete all your data at any time.

**Q: Is there a free trial?**
A: The Solo plan is free forever with basic features. The Pro plan includes a 14-day free trial with full access — no credit card required.

## 5. Content Principles

### Do

**Do speak in specifics.**
Specificity signals expertise and builds trust. "Engineers who sleep fewer than 6 hours show higher bug rates in code review" is more credible and more useful than "sleep affects your work performance." Every claim should have a level of detail that makes it feel real.

**Do name the audience explicitly.**
Use "engineers," "data scientists," "researchers," "writers" in headlines and body copy. Audience-specific language tells visitors instantly that this product is for them. Generic language signals a generic product.

**Do lead with the outcome, not the feature.**
Users do not buy features — they buy results. Lead with "Understand why some days you can't focus past noon" before explaining how the correlation engine works.

**Do make privacy claims concrete.**
For a health data product, vague privacy language is worse than no privacy language. "Your data stays yours" is meaningless. "Your individual sleep data is never visible to your manager, your employer, or any team admin — ever" is a claim. Make the claim.

**Do treat the reader as intelligent.**
This audience builds software, analyzes datasets, and reads technical documentation for fun. Do not over-explain. Trust them to follow a sentence with a dependent clause.

**Do use data to make points, not to show off.**
Numbers should serve arguments. "40% higher bug rate" earns its place because it makes the abstract concrete. A stat without a claim it supports is just decoration.

**Do write for dark mode.**
All copy should be reviewed in the actual dark-mode design context. Some phrasing that reads clearly on white backgrounds becomes different in feel against dark backgrounds. Test headlines visually, not just semantically.

### Do Not

**Do not use wellness industry language.**
Words and phrases to avoid: nourish, thrive, wellness journey, optimize your well-being, holistic, empower yourself, restore, rejuvenate. NightOwl is a performance tool, not a spa. The brand is intelligent and modern — wellness softness undermines it.

**Do not make unsubstantiated medical claims.**
NightOwl is not a medical device. Do not claim it diagnoses, treats, or prevents any condition. Do not imply that following recommendations will cure sleep disorders. The legal footer ("NightOwl does not provide medical advice") should reflect the product's actual positioning throughout the entire site, not just in fine print.

**Do not use fake urgency or manipulative scarcity.**
No countdown timers, no "limited spots available," no "offer ends soon" unless genuinely true. The target audience is deeply skeptical of these patterns and will disengage.

**Do not bury the integration story.**
The Integration Hub is a primary differentiator for this audience. Engineers and data scientists who already use GitHub and Linear need to see their tools named in the first two sections of the site — not just in the integrations section. Name the tools early.

**Do not write copy that works equally well for a different product.**
Every sentence on the site should be specific to NightOwl's proposition. If a headline or paragraph could appear on a generic productivity app, a meditation app, or a fitness tracker without modification, rewrite it.

**Do not use passive voice to soften accountability.**
"Insights are surfaced to help you make better decisions" is weaker than "NightOwl shows you exactly what last night's sleep means for your afternoon." Own the product's actions.

**Do not write FAQ answers that restate the question.**
"Great question! Sleep is very important..." is not an answer. Start FAQ answers with the actual answer in the first sentence, then provide context. The target audience will not read a paragraph-long preamble.

**Do not let Team Insights copy feel surveillance-adjacent.**
The Team plan is walking a delicate line. Copy must consistently and proactively address the privacy concern — not just in the FAQ, but in the feature description itself. Managers in this audience will have strong instincts against tools that feel panoptical. Lead with the aggregate-only, anonymized framing every single time Team Insights is mentioned.

## Appendix: Copy Review Checklist

Before any section goes live, review against these questions:

- [ ] Does this copy connect sleep to work performance in a specific way?
- [ ] Does it speak to at least one of the four target personas by role, tool, or pain point?
- [ ] Is every claim specific enough to be falsifiable (or at least falsifiable-feeling)?
- [ ] Does it avoid wellness-industry language and medical claims?
- [ ] Does it treat privacy as a feature, not a legal requirement?
- [ ] Could this exact copy appear on a competitor's site? (If yes, rewrite.)
- [ ] Does the CTA invite rather than demand?
- [ ] Is the reading level appropriate for a highly technical audience (aim for Flesch-Kincaid Grade 12+)?
- [ ] Has it been reviewed in dark mode at actual screen size?
